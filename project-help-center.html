The problem

One long page that asked before it helped.

The original contact flow put everything on a single scrolling page — game, platform, category, subcategory, suggested articles, a description box, and a submit button, all stacked together. There was no sense of progress, and the suggested articles sat passively in the middle of the page, easy to scroll straight past on the way to opening a ticket.

Worse, those article suggestions were static and category‑based — they didn't respond to what the player was actually describing. So the chance to answer a question before it became a ticket was mostly wasted.

Research

Testing the real flow against real scenarios.

I ran remote usability testing with 9 participants — casual gamers and non‑gamer parents of players, all on mobile — across seven real support scenarios: account recovery, two‑factor deactivation, account linking, in‑game purchases, toxic‑behaviour reporting, a game crash, and a data‑deletion request.

Each scenario measured how easily someone could complete and submit a case. Account linking scored lowest, which pointed straight at where the structure was breaking down.

Three problems, framed as questions

Synthesizing the findings (Experiments → Facts → Insights → Opportunities) surfaced three clear directions, each framed as a How Might We:

HMW 01 · Hierarchy

Improve visual hierarchy to support task success and scanning.

Missing titles and labels left users unsure what to do or what to provide — for example, whether a file upload was expected.

HMW 02 · Login

Make it clear when users actually have to log in.

Forcing login at the moment of submitting felt counterintuitive, and made users think they'd need to log in every single time they wanted help.

HMW 03 · Transitions

Make the move between steps smooth and clear.

Selecting an option triggered a transition that "felt like a glitch," giving no meaningful feedback about what had just happened.

The solution

A guided flow that helps before it asks.

I restructured the single page into a four‑step flow with a visible stepper, so players always know where they are and what's left. Each research problem mapped to a specific design decision — and the flow was rebuilt around an active AI step that resolves issues before they become tickets.

Hierarchy

Clear layout & guidance

Straightforward structure, real titles and labels, and informational messages where users were getting lost.

Login

Honest about login

Tell users login is needed to submit a case — but let them browse suggested answers without logging in at all.

Transitions

Progressive disclosure

Separate, deliberate steps that reveal information as needed, so each transition feels intentional, not broken.

AI deflection

An AI answer in the path

An AI help step placed before ticket creation, so players get a relevant answer first — turning passive article cards into active, in‑flow resolution.

The flow, step by step

How the four steps move a player from problem to resolution.

Step 1 · Problem with

One clear starting point.

Players choose a single entry point — a game, a service, or a problem type — instead of facing every field at once. One decision, no overwhelm.

Step 2 · Provide details

Confirm the details, editable inline.

Platform, category, and sub‑category are gathered and shown back clearly, each changeable in place — so nothing feels locked in by mistake.

Step 3 · AI help

An answer before a ticket.

Before creating a ticket, players describe their issue and get an AI‑generated answer with relevant help articles and a "was this helpful?" check. The AI itself was built by a dedicated team; my role was designing how it lived in the flow — where it sits, how the answer and disclosure are presented, and how the feedback loop works — with honest messaging that AI can get things wrong.

Step 4 · Create ticket

If still needed, a complete report.

Only if the AI answer didn't resolve it do players open a ticket — now pre‑filled with everything gathered, plus file upload. Reports arrive structured and complete, so support teams get what they need the first time.
