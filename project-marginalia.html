Research

Four patterns kept surfacing.

01

Choice overload, thin info

Millions of titles, but only a blurb and a star average to decide by.

02

People trust people

A strong preference for a recommendation from a person over a machine.

M

Meet Maya — the persona

Maya, a curious cross-format reader who browses far more than she buys, needs a way to discover and trust books beyond the bestseller list, in order to feel the delight of finding something genuinely worth reading.

The concept

Three moves, one idea.

Run through How-Might-We questions and brainwriting, stress-tested against Maya, and prioritized on an importance/difficulty matrix, the concept resolved into three connected moves.

Move 1 · Discovery is the front door

Enter by mood, not by scroll.

No infinite scroll, no bestseller treadmill. Readers wander in by feeling — a mood, a hidden-gem filter, or a blind date — instead of facing a wall of everything.

Move 2 · Curators are the trust layer

Follow a person, not an algorithm.

Real people with personalities and taste — the cozy one, the strange one, the fast one, the deep one — woven through every screen, followable rather than gated behind a paywall.

Move 3 · Numbers + a human, together

The star says "liked." The note says "for you."

Keep the familiar star rating shoppers expect — but always pair it with a named curator's note at equal weight. The star answers "is this generally liked?"; the human answers "is this for me?"

Signature feature

Blind date with a book.

Engineered serendipity: the cover stays hidden, and the book is sold on three honest words and a curator's personal pitch — recreating the bookshop-rummage thrill. You add it to your bag without ever seeing the cover, or reveal it if you lose your nerve.

Measuring success

Every bet has a metric that could disprove it.

The point isn't to track everything — it's to measure whether the specific bets paid off. Each design bet is tied to a signal, including the one that would tell us we're wrong.

The betThe metricWhat would disprove it
Human curation beats algorithms
Curator-attributed conversion vs. search conversion
If curator visits convert no better than search, the premise is weak.
Mood-based discovery resonates
Mood-door click-through and completion
If users ignore moods and default to search, the front door is wrong.
Rating + curator note builds trust
Note engagement and curator follow rate
If people only read the star and never the note, the pairing isn't earning its place.
Blind date adds delight
Open rate, accept-without-reveal, return usage
If it's opened once and never again, it's a gimmick, not a feature.

Guardrail metrics — return rate, save-to-purchase lag, refund rate — keep an engagement win from hiding a conversion or trust problem. The trap to avoid is vanity metrics: time-on-site and page views can look healthy while readers are actually lost, so every signal here is tied to a hypothesis, not just activity.

Reflection

Designing trust, not just an interface.

The hard part of Marginalia wasn't the screens — it was making a stranger's recommendation feel trustworthy enough to act on. Community features (reader clubs, curator replies, curators earning a cut) tested as valuable but heavy, so they were deliberately parked as a labelled future-vision phase rather than overloading v1. Restraint was part of the design.
