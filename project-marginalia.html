The problem

Recommendation engines are good at sales, bad at taste.

Most book discovery online is algorithmic: "because you read X, here's Y." It's efficient and soulless — optimised for what sells, not for what you'd actually love. The serendipity of a good bookshop, where a knowledgeable human hands you something you'd never have picked, is exactly what the algorithm strips out. Marginalia is a concept that asks: what if discovery was built around human curation and mood, not a recommendation engine?

Who it's for

The reader who misses the bookshop.

M

The mood-led reader

Reads widely, trusts recommendations from people over machines, and often shops by feeling — "I want something cosy," "something that'll wreck me" — rather than by genre or bestseller list. Frustrated by algorithmic sameness and decision fatigue.

The concept

Three ways in, all human.

Marginalia replaces the recommendation engine with three discovery paths, each built around feeling and human taste rather than purchase history.

01 · Mood

Enter by feeling

Start from a mood rather than a genre — discovery shaped around how you want to feel, not what you bought before.

02 · Curators

Follow real people

Follow human curators with actual taste instead of an algorithm — discovery you can trust because there's a person behind it.

03 · Blind date

Blind date with a book

A book sold on its vibe — a few evocative lines, no cover, no title — until you commit to the feeling first.

04 · Margins

Notes in the margins

The namesake idea — curators' marginalia, the handwritten-feeling notes that make a recommendation personal.

Signature feature

A blind date with a book.

The feature I'm most attached to: a book presented only by its mood and a few lines of voice — no cover, no title, no author. You choose it on feeling alone, the way a friend might say "just trust me on this one." It's the clearest expression of the whole concept: taste over metadata.
